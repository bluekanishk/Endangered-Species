Glossary Terms: P to R

P

 

pampas    A huge, usually grass-covered area found in South America.

parasites    Animals or plants that live and/or feed on other animals or plants to survive, causing harm, such as ticks feeding on the blood of cattle.

permeable    A material that soaks up liquid or gases, or a material that liquid and gases can pass through.

pesticides    Chemicals used on crops and other plants to kill insects that can damage the crops.

photosynthesis    The process by which green plants, including algae, can use sunlight to turn water, and carbon dioxide, into food and oxygen.

phytoplankton    Minuscule plants that float in water and that are at the beginning of the food chain.

pinnipeds    Mammals, such as seals and walruses, that have limbs like flippers that are adapted for underwater use.

plankton    Tiny organisms, such as plants and animals, that live just under or on the surface of water and that are eaten by larger animals, such as fish and whales.

plantation    A large area where a crop is grown, such as tobacco, coffee, or cotton.

pollutants    (ocean) Anything that goes into the water that can harm the animals that live in it. Pollutants include chemicals, oil, garbage, and sewage.

polyps    The individual parts of a coral, each polyp consists of an invertebrate animal that has a cylindrical body with a ring of tentacles around the mouth.

pupate    To change into a pupa from a larva; the pupa is a stage in the life cycle of an insect. The adult insect emerges from the pupa.

prairies    Large area of treeless grassland in North America.

predator    An animal that hunts and kills another animal for food.

prey    An animal that is hunted and killed as food by another animal.

primates    Mammals that have well-developed brains, including lemurs, monkeys, apes, orangutans, gorillas, and humans.

pseudo-thumbs    A bone that is used by an animal as a thumb for grasping. For example, giant pandas use a pseudo-thumb to grasp bamboo shoots.

Q

 

quotas    A set amount that has been agreed by everyone concerned; for fishing quotas, the amount of fish by weight that each country or area can catch.

R

 

rabies    An infectious, deadly disease common among animals, such as dogs, cats, and wolves. In some parts of the world, rabies can be passed on to humans. It is easily controlled by vaccination.

radio tagging    When a small piece of equipment is attached to an animal (usually as a collar) so that conservationists can track the animal and learn about its range and behavior. The radio tagging does not harm the animal in any way and is usually removed after a certain time period.

Ramsar sites    Important wetland areas that have been identified by the Convention on Wetlands of International Importance (Ramsar Convention), an international treaty signed in Ramsar, Iran, to protect the world's wetlands.

raptors    Birds of prey, such as eagles, hawks, or falcons.

Red List    A list compiled by the IUCN, International Union for the Conservation of Nature, that uses precise scientific data to record the endangered status of animals in the wild. It is updated every 5 to 10 years.

refugees    People who are forced out of their country and have to live in another country because of war or natural disasters.

regurgitate    To bring up food that has been eaten and partly digested.

rehabilitation    When wild animals that have been held captive or injured are taught to live in the wild again.

reservoirs    Huge lakes of water that have been created by humans.

retractable claws    Claws that can be pulled into a protective sheath, and that spring out to catch prey. Wild and domestic cats have retractable claws.

Rinderpest    A highly infectious, deadly disease that affects cattle and that is caused by a virus.

rodent    A small or medium-sized mammal, such as a beaver, rat, or squirrel that has teeth for nibbling and gnawing.

roost    A roost is a place where winged animals, such as birds and bats, gather too sleep and rest. It is usually off the ground and in a sheltered place. A roost is also the name of a group of sleeping or resting winged animals or birds.
