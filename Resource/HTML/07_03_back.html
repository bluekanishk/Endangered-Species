Glossary Terms: D to F

D

 

deciduous    Trees that lose their leaves for part of the year, for example oak and elm trees. They drop their leaves in the fall, and grow new leaves in the spring.

deforestation    When huge areas of trees are cut down for commercial reasons, such as to make space for housing, or to use the wood to sell. Replacement trees are not replanted so the forest is destroyed forever.

delicacy    An expensive food usually eaten on special occasions.

dew claw    A fifth toe with a claw found on the inside of the front legs of dogs. It has little function in most dogs.

dieback    When plants die from the outer edges inward, for example from the tips of the branches toward the trunk. Dieback can be caused by disease, lack of water, and by some insects.

distemper    An infectious disease passed among dogs. Unless it is treated the dogs become very ill and die.

dorsal fin    The large fin on the back of some fish. It helps the animals to keep their balance as they move through the water.

dominant    The leader or strongest member in a group. In some groups the dominant female is the only one that mates and has young.

drift net    A large fishing net, sometimes stretching for hundreds of miles (kilometers) that floats from the top of the ocean downward and is carried along by the tide.

droppings    The solid poo, faeces, or waste left by an animal.

E

 

Ebola disease    A virus that causes death in both humans and animals, especially monkeys. It is found mainly in Africa.

echolocation    The way in which some animals, such as dolphins and bats, can "see" what is around them. They send out high-pitched sounds that bounce off any objects ahead and an echo travels back to them.

ecotourism    A form of tourism that involves visiting natural places, such as National Parks, without causing harm to the environment, and at the same time benefiting the local people.

eroded    When rocks or coastlines are worn away by wind or water.

European Union (EU)    An organization of countries in Europe that have joined together for political and economic reasons.

excrement    The solid poo, faeces, or waste left by an animal.

F

 

feral    An animal that was once domesticated but that has returned to being wild.

fertilized    When an egg from a female is joined by the male's sperm to create an embryo, the beginning of a new life.

filter feeders    Animals that live in the water that have a sieve-like mouth part that can separate solid pieces of food from the water, such as the humpback whale.

fishing quotas    The amount of fish (by weight) commercial fishing boats can catch over a period of time in certain parts of the world.

flukes    The two halves of the ends of a whale's tail that spread out like wings.

fluked tail    A tail that has an end divided into two, like a "v" shape.

fossa    A small cat-like, meat-eating mammal found in Madagascar.

foot-and-mouth    A very infectious disease that usually affects cattle and other hoofed livestock.

forage    To hunt on the ground for food.

fragmented    When parts of a large forest area or other habitat has been destroyed so that only small pockets of the habitat remain, and these are isolated from each other.
